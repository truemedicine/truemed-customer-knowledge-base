## The Short Answer

Yes, you can use your HSA or FSA to pay for qualified medical expenses on behalf of your spouse and eligible dependents. You do not need separate accounts for each family member, and in most cases your existing account covers everyone in your household who qualifies.

---

## Who Counts as a Dependent?

For HSA and FSA purposes, the IRS recognizes three categories of people you can spend on behalf of:

**Your spouse**
If you are married, you can use your HSA or FSA to pay for your spouse's qualified medical expenses, even if your spouse is not enrolled in your health plan.

**Qualifying children**
A child qualifies as a dependent if they meet the IRS definition, which generally means they are:

- Under age 19 at the end of the tax year (or under age 24 if a full-time student)
- Living with you for more than half the year
- Not providing more than half of their own financial support
- Not filing a joint tax return (with limited exceptions)

**Qualifying relatives**
In some cases, other individuals you financially support — such as a parent or sibling — may qualify as a dependent under IRS rules. If they meet the criteria for a qualifying relative, you can generally use your HSA or FSA for their eligible medical expenses as well.

<Note>
Tax dependency rules can be nuanced. If you are unsure whether someone qualifies as your dependent for HSA/FSA purposes, consult a tax advisor or review IRS Publication 502.
</Note>

## How It Works in Practice

**HSA**
You can use your HSA funds for your own expenses, your spouse's expenses, or any qualifying dependent's expenses — even if that person is not covered under your High-Deductible Health Plan. The funds simply need to be spent on a qualified medical expense for an eligible individual.

**Health Care FSA**
The same rules apply: your FSA can be used for eligible medical expenses for you, your spouse, and your qualifying dependents. There is no need to set up separate accounts.

Only the account holder (or their spouse on a shared FSA) can make spending decisions from the account. Dependents cannot access or spend from your account directly.

---

## Can a Dependent Have Their Own Account?

**Children under your care**
A child claimed as your tax dependent generally cannot open or contribute to their own HSA. HSA eligibility requires enrollment in a qualifying HDHP, and a dependent who is covered under a parent's plan while still being claimed as a tax dependent does not meet the IRS criteria to hold their own HSA.

Once a child is no longer claimed as a tax dependent — typically after they become financially independent — they can open their own HSA if they are enrolled in a qualifying HDHP.

**Spouses**
If both you and your spouse have access to employer-sponsored benefits, you can each hold your own HSA or FSA. This effectively doubles your household's tax-advantaged health spending capacity. If you both have HSAs with family HDHP coverage, your combined contributions cannot exceed the family limit ($8,550 in 2025).

## Using Truemed for Dependent Purchases

Truemed can be used for purchases that are medically appropriate for a qualifying dependent, not just for the account holder. The key requirement is that the purchase is medically necessary for the specific individual it is intended to benefit.

Here is how the process works for a dependent purchase:

1. Complete the health intake form on your dependent's behalf, providing their health information rather than your own
2. An independent, licensed clinician reviews the intake and determines whether the purchase is medically necessary for that individual
3. If it qualifies, an LMN is issued and you pay using your HSA or FSA card as normal

The LMN documents the medical necessity for the specific person the purchase is for, which is what makes the transaction IRS-compliant. Using an LMN issued for your own health conditions to cover a dependent's purchase would not be appropriate.

<Note>
Truemed does issue LMNs for qualifying dependent purchases, including for minors. The account holder completes the intake on the dependent's behalf. If you have questions about a specific dependent purchase, contact support@truemed.com.
</Note>

## Common Questions

**Can I use my HSA/FSA for my adult child who is still on my health insurance?**
It depends on their tax status. Under the Affordable Care Act, you can keep a child on your health insurance until age 26. However, for HSA/FSA purposes, the child must qualify as your tax dependent. An adult child who files their own taxes or supports themselves financially generally does not qualify as your tax dependent, even if they remain on your health plan.

**My spouse has their own HSA. Can we each spend on the other?**
Yes. Each spouse can use their own HSA for the other's qualified medical expenses.

**Can I use my Dependent Care FSA for my child's medical expenses?**
No. A Dependent Care FSA covers childcare costs only (daycare, preschool, after-school programs). For a child's medical expenses, use your Health Care FSA or HSA instead.
